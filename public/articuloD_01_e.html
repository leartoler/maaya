The Game of Patolli: Between the Civic and the Religious

The Patolli board, which has been found in various regions such as Teotihuacan, Tula, and in the Maya lowlands, like the one found in structure T7_44279, is a game played on a cruciform board. It is associated with religious symbolism and the calendar.

It is important to remember that for the ancient Maya, aspects of the calendar were strongly associated with their worldview, so the true meaning of what the game represents is still debated. For example, in a study conducted by Swezey (1983), it is suggested that the game, which is mostly observed in the Borgia Codex or the Vaticanus Codex, is associated with Xiuhmolpilli and Macuilxóchitl. It was indeed a portable ball game played with dice. Furthermore, Swezey explains that each player had to pass through fifty-two squares, and after every thirteenth square, the player would reach a "safe" or "punished" square, symbolized by a double triangle or a cross, which reminded of the Ollin sign.

However, another detail that is still debated is whether the patollis represented in the codices and in the central highlands are the same as those found in graffiti or incised designs, such as the one on monument T7_44279. One proposal is that those found in graffiti or incised designs are proto-patollis. However, both display common elements, such as symbolism that may refer to the four cardinal points, which could be represented by a flower.

Regarding its timeline, records of the game are found in Teotihuacan during the middle and late periods (450 - 750 AD), which correspond to the Late Classic period in the Maya region. Therefore, a connection can be observed with the one found on monument T7_44279, which, according to Saucedo, dates to 600-900 AD.

Religious and civic character.

One notable aspect of the patolli found in Front 3 of Section 7 is that it was discovered in a civic context, which would imply, as Dena Castro mentioned, that high-ranking individuals gathered in this area, and that it was likely used for mediation or recreation, aside from political discourses. This adds to the debate of whether patollis were solely religious and calendrical in nature, as previously mentioned. Without a doubt, this important discovery adds to the ongoing discussion regarding one of the most significant Mesoamerican games for which we have information.
